FACILITATION CHEAT SHEET — Cafeteria Compostable Trays

TIMING (50-min period)
  Phase 1: ~12 min  |  Phase 2: ~10 min  |  Phase 3: ~13 min  |  Phase 4: ~15 min

WHAT TO EXPECT
  Flaw 1: "They're saying a lot based on very little" — turn 3 (Maya)
    Students should notice: Maya says 'the research proves that compostable trays are the best option for any school' but she only read one article
    Most will catch this.

  Flaw 2: "That's just one example" — turn 5 (Maya)
    Students should notice: Maya mentions one school in Oregon and says 'if it worked there, it'll work at our school too'
    Most will catch this.

  Flaw 3: "They only talked about the good stuff" — turn 7 (Maya)
    Students should notice: Maya says 'none of the sources I found said anything bad' and concludes there are no real problems
    Harder to spot.

  Flaw 4: "Someone raised a good point but gave up on it" — turns 8-10 (Jordan)
    Students should notice: Jordan says 'I don't want to be the one slowing us down' and drops all his earlier questions about cost and cafeteria size
    Harder to spot.

  Flaw 5: "They're claiming way more than they showed" — turn 9 (Maya)
    Students should notice: Maya calls compostable trays 'a proven solution' based on one article and one school
    Easy to miss.

PHASE 1: Student isn't finding flaws
→ "Re-read turn 3 carefully. Maya says the research 'proves' something. Ask yourself: how much research did she actually do?"
→ "Look at turn 5. Maya talks about a school in Oregon. Is one school enough to know something will work at every school?"
→ "Follow Jordan's turns from turn 4 all the way to turn 10. He asks some really good questions early on. What happens to those questions by the end?"
→ "Re-read turns 7 and 9. Pay attention to how Maya talks about problems and downsides. Does she think there are any?"
→ "Look at turn 9. Maya calls compostable trays 'a proven solution.' Go back and check — how much proof did she actually give? Does what she showed match how sure she sounds?"

PHASE 2: Student found flaw but stuck on thinking behavior
→ For "They're saying a lot based on very little": Narrow to 3: "They only paid attention to things that agreed with them" / "They got stuck on the first thing they heard and couldn't let go" / "They only focused on one part and missed the bigger picture"
→ "Imagine you're Maya. You found an article last night that really excited you about compostable trays. You're ready to present it to the class. Why might you say 'the research proves it' when you've only read one article?"
→ For "That's just one example": Narrow to 3: "They only looked at sources and opinions that agreed with them" / "They only paid attention to things that agreed with them" / "They only focused on one part and missed the bigger picture"
→ "Imagine you're Maya and you found a really impressive number — 40 percent less waste! That's exciting. Why might you assume that one school's success means it'll work at your school too?"
→ For "They only talked about the good stuff": Narrow to 3: "They only looked at sources and opinions that agreed with them" / "They only paid attention to things that agreed with them" / "They acted like it was either totally great or totally terrible"
→ "Imagine you searched for information and every result you found was positive. Would you think 'maybe I'm not searching the right way' or would you think 'see, there really aren't any problems'? What might make someone think the second one?"
→ For "Someone raised a good point but gave up on it": Narrow to 3: "They just went along with everyone else instead of thinking for themselves" / "They believed it because of who said it, not because the evidence was good" / "They believed it because they felt strongly, not because they had proof"
→ "Imagine you're Jordan. You have some good questions, but Maya has done more research than you and the presentation deadline is coming up. How would you feel about keeping on pushing back?"
→ For "They're claiming way more than they showed": Narrow to 3: "They only paid attention to things that agreed with them" / "They believed it because they felt strongly, not because they had proof" / "They only focused on one part and missed the bigger picture"
→ "Imagine you're Maya at the end of the conversation. You feel good about your research and you're ready to present. Why might you call something 'proven' even though you only have one real piece of data?"

PHASE 3: Group discussion is stalled [same for every scenario]
→ "Did you all mark the same turns? Look at where you differ."
→ "Someone in your group found something you missed. Take another look."

PHASE 4: Class isn't engaging with AI perspective
→ "Some of you noticed that Maya says 'the research proves it' but she only read one article. That's a great catch — you spotted that a big claim needs big evidence to back it up."
→ "The AI says Maya was in an echo chamber — only finding sources that agreed with her. But some of you might think she was just doing normal research and happened to find positive results. Both sides — what's your argument?"
→ "Let's look at Jordan for a second. In turns 4 and 6, he asks some really smart questions — about cost, about cafeteria size, about whether one example is enough. But by turn 8, he stops pushing. What changed? Why did he give up on those questions?"
→ "The AI thinks Jordan gave in because of groupthink — he didn't want to slow the team down. But you could also argue he just trusted Maya because she did more research. Which do you think it was, and why?"
→ "In turn 9, Maya says compostable trays are 'a proven solution.' Think about what they actually have as evidence — one article and one school. Is that enough to call something 'proven'? What would you need to see before you used that word?"
