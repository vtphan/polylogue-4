FACILITATION CHEAT SHEET — Ocean Plastic Pollution Awareness Campaign

TIMING (50-min period)
  Phase 1: ~10 min  |  Phase 2: ~10 min  |  Phase 3: ~15 min  |  Phase 4: ~15 min

WHAT TO EXPECT
  Flaw 1: "They're saying a lot based on very little" — turn 5 (Mia)
    Students should notice: Mia says "the documentary and the article both prove" and
    "it's not even a question anymore" based on just two sources.
    Most will catch this.

  Flaw 2: "How would that actually work?" — turns 9-11 (Jaylen, with Mia adding on)
    Students should notice: Jaylen describes stations, displays, art projects, social
    media, and a sculpture — but never mentions cost, time, materials, or who would help.
    Most will catch this.

  Flaw 3: "That's just one example" — turn 5 (Mia)
    Students should notice: Mia says one town switching to reusable bags "proves
    awareness campaigns work."
    Harder to spot.

  Flaw 4: "That's not a strong enough source for that claim" — turn 7 (Mia)
    Students should notice: Mia says "I already have a real article with real statistics
    and a whole documentary — that's pretty solid."
    Harder to spot.

  Flaw 5: "Someone raised a good point but gave up on it" — turns 6-8 (Jaylen)
    Students should notice: Jaylen asks "should we find a few more sources?" but gives
    up after Mia says they have enough.
    Easy to miss.

  Flaw 6: "They're claiming way more than they showed" — turn 12 (Mia)
    Students should notice: Mia says the campaign will "totally change how people think
    about plastic" and that they have "the research and a real plan."
    Harder to spot.

PHASE 1: Student isn't finding flaws
→ "Re-read turn 5. Mia says something 'proves' that awareness campaigns work. How many
   examples does she give? Is that enough to prove it?"
→ "Look at turns 9 through 11. Jaylen is describing a lot of cool activities. Now ask
   yourself: could they actually pull all of this off? What would they need?"
→ "Follow Jaylen's turns in order. In turn 6, he asks a question. What happens to that
   question? Does it ever get answered?"
→ "Read the last turn — turn 12. Mia says they have 'the research and a real plan.' Go
   back and count: how many sources did they actually find?"

PHASE 2: Student found flaw but stuck on thinking behavior
→ For "big claim, little evidence": Narrow to 3: "Only paid attention to things that
   agreed with them" / "Believed it because they felt strongly" / "Only looked at
   sources that agreed with them"
→ "Imagine you're Mia. You watched a documentary about sea turtles that made you almost
   cry, and then you found an article with a huge number — eight million tons. Why might
   you say those two sources 'prove' something?"

→ For "missing practical details": Narrow to 3: "Only focused on one part and missed
   the bigger picture" / "Believed it because they felt strongly" / "Just went along
   with everyone else"
→ "Imagine you're Jaylen. You just came up with this awesome idea for an awareness day
   with stations and art projects and a giant sea turtle sculpture. Why might you skip
   over questions like 'how much would this cost?'"

PHASE 3: Group discussion is stalled [same for every scenario]
→ "Did you all mark the same turns? Look at where you differ."
→ "Someone in your group found something you missed. Take another look."

PHASE 4: Class isn't engaging with AI perspective
→ "Nobody mentioned this yet — in turn 6, Jaylen asks if they should find more sources.
   What happens to that question? Did it ever get a real answer?"
→ "Some of you noticed that Mia only has two sources but talks like she has a mountain
   of proof. Nice catch. Why do you think she felt so confident with just two sources?"
→ "A lot of you spotted that Jaylen's plan sounds really cool but is missing some big
   practical details. What specific details would they need to figure out?"
→ "The AI says Mia might be showing 'confirmation bias.' But some of you might think
   she's just passionate and excited. Both sides — make your case."
→ "The AI says Jaylen dropped a good question because of 'groupthink.' But maybe he
   just decided it wasn't worth arguing about. What do you think?"
