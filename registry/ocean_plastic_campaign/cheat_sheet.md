FACILITATION CHEAT SHEET — Ocean Plastic Campaign

TIMING (50-min period)
  Phase 1: ~10 min  |  Phase 2: ~10 min  |  Phase 3: ~15 min  |  Phase 4: ~15 min

WHAT TO EXPECT
  Flaw 1: "They're saying a lot based on very little" — turn 5 (Mia)
    Students should notice: Mia says 'the documentary and the article both prove' and 'it's not even a question anymore' based on just two sources
    Most will catch this.

  Flaw 2: "That's not a strong enough source for that claim" — turn 7 (Mia)
    Students should notice: Mia says 'I already have a real article with real statistics and a whole documentary — that's pretty solid'
    Harder to spot.

  Flaw 3: "That's just one example" — turn 5 (Mia)
    Students should notice: Mia says one town switching to reusable bags 'proves awareness campaigns work'
    Harder to spot.

  Flaw 4: "How would that actually work?" — turns 9-11 (Jaylen, with Mia adding on)
    Students should notice: Jaylen describes stations, displays, art projects, social media, and a sculpture — but never mentions cost, time, materials, or who would help
    Most will catch this.

  Flaw 5: "Someone raised a good point but gave up on it" — turns 6-8 (Jaylen raises it, Mia dismisses it)
    Students should notice: Jaylen asks 'should we find a few more sources?' but gives up after Mia says they have enough
    Easy to miss.

  Flaw 6: "They're claiming way more than they showed" — turn 12 (Mia)
    Students should notice: Mia says the campaign will 'totally change how people think about plastic' and that they have 'the research and a real plan'
    Harder to spot.

PHASE 1: Student isn't finding flaws
→ "Re-read turn 5. Mia says something 'proves' that awareness campaigns work. How many examples does she give? Is that enough to prove it?"
→ "Re-read turn 5 again — Mia mentions one town that switched to reusable bags. Does one town prove something works everywhere?"
→ "Look at turns 9 through 11. Jaylen is describing a lot of cool activities. Now ask yourself: could they actually pull all of this off? What would they need?"
→ "Follow Jaylen's turns in order. In turn 6, he asks a question. What happens to that question? Does it ever get answered?"
→ "Read the last turn — turn 12. Mia says they have 'the research and a real plan.' Go back and count: how many sources did they actually find? And does their plan cover how they'd actually do everything?"

PHASE 2: Student found flaw but stuck on thinking behavior
→ For "They're saying a lot based on very little": Narrow to 3: "They only paid attention to things that agreed with them" / "They believed it because they felt strongly, not because they had proof" / "They only looked at sources and opinions that agreed with them"
→ "Imagine you're Mia. You watched a documentary about sea turtles that made you almost cry, and then you found an article with a huge number — eight million tons. You feel really strongly that this matters. Why might you say those two sources 'prove' something?"
→ For "How would that actually work?": Narrow to 3: "They only focused on one part and missed the bigger picture" / "They believed it because they felt strongly, not because they had proof" / "They just went along with everyone else instead of thinking for themselves"
→ "Imagine you're Jaylen. You just came up with this awesome idea for an awareness day with stations and art projects and a giant sea turtle sculpture. You're so excited picturing it. Why might you skip over questions like 'how much would this cost' or 'who's going to help?'"
→ For "Someone raised a good point but gave up on it": Narrow to 3: "They just went along with everyone else instead of thinking for themselves" / "They believed it because of who said it, not because the evidence was good" / "They only focused on one part and missed the bigger picture"
→ "Imagine you're Jaylen. You think maybe they should find more sources, but Mia sounds really confident and says two is enough. You also really want to get to the fun part — planning the campaign. Why might you just say 'yeah okay' and move on?"
→ For "That's just one example": Narrow to 3: "They only paid attention to things that agreed with them" / "They got stuck on the first thing they heard and couldn't let go" / "They believed it because they felt strongly, not because they had proof"
→ "Imagine you're Mia. You watched this documentary and there was this one story about a town that switched to reusable bags, and it was really inspiring. Why might you take that one story and say it proves the whole idea works?"
→ For "They're claiming way more than they showed": Narrow to 3: "They believed it because they felt strongly, not because they had proof" / "They only paid attention to things that agreed with them" / "They only focused on one part and missed the bigger picture"
→ "Imagine you're Mia at the end of the discussion. You and Jaylen just had this really exciting brainstorm and you're feeling great about the project. Why might you say it's going to 'totally change' how people think, even though you haven't figured out all the details yet?"
→ For "That's not a strong enough source for that claim": Narrow to 3: "They only paid attention to things that agreed with them" / "They believed it because of who said it, not because the evidence was good" / "They believed it because they felt strongly, not because they had proof"
→ "Imagine you're Mia. Someone asks if you need more sources, and you think, 'I have a real article with real statistics AND a documentary — that's two whole sources.' Why might you feel like that's solid enough?"

PHASE 3: Group discussion is stalled [same for every scenario]
→ "Did you all mark the same turns? Look at where you differ."
→ "Someone in your group found something you missed. Take another look."

PHASE 4: Class isn't engaging with AI perspective
→ "Nobody mentioned this yet — in turn 6, Jaylen asks if they should find more sources. What happens to that question? Did it ever get a real answer, or did it just disappear?"
→ "Let's look at the very end — turn 12. Mia says this plan is going to 'totally change' how people think about plastic. Based on everything they actually have — one article, one documentary, and a plan with no details about cost or timing — is that a fair thing to say?"
→ "Some of you noticed that Mia only has two sources but talks like she has a mountain of proof. Nice catch. Let's dig into that — why do you think she felt so confident with just two sources?"
→ "A lot of you spotted that Jaylen's plan sounds really cool but is missing some big practical details. Great work. What specific details would they need to figure out before this plan could actually happen?"
→ "The AI says Mia might be showing 'confirmation bias' — only paying attention to things that agree with her. But some of you might think she's just really passionate and excited. Both sides — make your case. Can someone be passionate AND still be thinking clearly?"
→ "The AI says Jaylen dropped a good question in turn 6 because of 'groupthink' — just going along with Mia. But maybe he just decided it wasn't worth arguing about. What do you think — was he going along to get along, or did he make a reasonable choice to move on?"
