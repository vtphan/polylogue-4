FACILITATION CHEAT SHEET — Wolf River Greenway Trees

TIMING (50-min period)
  Phase 1: ~12 min  |  Phase 2: ~10 min  |  Phase 3: ~13 min  |  Phase 4: ~15 min

WHAT TO EXPECT
  Flaw 1: "That's not why it happens" — turns 3 and 5 (Jayla)
    Students should notice: Jayla says trees cool the neighborhood by absorbing CO2, calling it 'the root cause of why it's hot' — but CO2 absorption works on a global scale over decades, not on one street on a hot day
    Harder to spot.

  Flaw 2: "Someone raised a good point but gave up on it" — turn 6 (DeShawn)
    Students should notice: DeShawn says 'your explanation sounds more scientific' and 'I don't know enough to argue with it' even though his question in turn 4 was never actually answered
    Most will catch this.

  Flaw 3: "They just wanted everyone to feel included, not to get it right" — turn 8 (DeShawn)
    Students should notice: DeShawn says 'I still kinda think shade is doing more' but then 'I don't want to hold us up, so let's just use your idea'
    Most will catch this.

PHASE 1: Student isn't finding flaws
→ "Re-read turns 3 and 5 with just this question: Is Jayla giving the right reason for why trees cool things down, or is she mixing up two different things?"
→ "Follow DeShawn's turns from start to finish. Does he change his mind because Jayla convinced him, or for some other reason?"
→ "Look at turn 4 where DeShawn asks his question. Does anyone ever actually answer it?"
→ "Read the last two turns. Does DeShawn really agree, or is something else going on?"

PHASE 2: Student found flaw but stuck on thinking behavior
→ For "That's not why it happens": Narrow to 3: "They only focused on one part and missed the bigger picture" / "They only paid attention to things that agreed with them" / "They got stuck on the first thing they heard and couldn't let go"
→ "Imagine you're Jayla. You just learned about CO2 and the greenhouse effect and it all clicked — trees absorb CO2, CO2 causes warming, so trees must fix the warming! Why would it be hard to consider that shade might actually matter more for cooling a neighborhood?"
→ For "Someone raised a good point but gave up on it": Narrow to 3: "They believed it because of who said it, not because the evidence was good" / "They just went along with everyone else instead of thinking for themselves" / "They believed it because they felt strongly, not because they had proof"
→ "Imagine you're DeShawn. You asked a really good question and you know shade feels cooler — but Jayla keeps using words like 'greenhouse effect' and 'CO2 absorption' that sound very scientific. Why might you start doubting yourself even though your question was never answered?"
→ For "They just wanted everyone to feel included, not to get it right": Narrow to 2: "They just went along with everyone else instead of thinking for themselves" / "They believed it because they felt strongly, not because they had proof"
→ "Imagine you're DeShawn at the end of the discussion. You still think you might be right about shade, but the group needs to finish. Why would you go along with an answer you're not sure about?"

PHASE 3: Group discussion is stalled [same for every scenario]
→ "Did you all mark the same turns? Look at where you differ."
→ "Someone in your group found something you missed. Take another look."

PHASE 4: Class isn't engaging with AI perspective
→ "Let's look at something the AI noticed. In turns 3 and 5, Jayla says trees cool the neighborhood by absorbing CO2. The AI thinks she might be giving the wrong reason — that CO2 absorption is a real thing, but it works on a global scale, not on one street. What do you think? Is the AI right, or does Jayla have a point?"
→ "A lot of you noticed that DeShawn gave up on his idea even though nobody proved him wrong. The AI flagged the same thing. Nice work — that's a really important thing to notice in group discussions. Why do you think it matters when someone drops a good point?"
→ "The AI says Jayla had 'tunnel vision' — she got so focused on CO2 that she couldn't see other explanations. Some of you might have said she had 'confirmation bias' instead — she only paid attention to evidence that agreed with her. Both sides, make your case. What's the difference, and which one fits better here?"
→ "The AI says DeShawn backed down because Jayla's explanation 'sounded more scientific.' Do you think that's fair? Can you think of a time when something sounded really smart but turned out to be wrong? Or a time when a simple explanation was actually the right one?"
→ "At the very end, DeShawn says 'I don't want to hold us up, so let's just use your idea.' The AI thinks he agreed to keep the peace, not because he was convinced. Have you ever done that in a group project? What happens when a group picks an answer just to be done?"
