FACILITATION CHEAT SHEET — Deforestation Reforestation

TIMING (50-min period)
  Phase 1: ~12 min  |  Phase 2: ~10 min  |  Phase 3: ~13 min  |  Phase 4: ~15 min

WHAT TO EXPECT
  Flaw 1: "They only talked about the good stuff" — turns 5-8 (Sofia)
    Students should notice: Sofia lists benefit after benefit — jobs, volunteers, wildlife — and says 'I don't see any downsides.' She never once mentions a limitation even after Marcus asks about the 20-year timeline.
    Most will catch this.

  Flaw 2: "Someone raised a good point but gave up on it" — turns 11 and 13 (Marcus)
    Students should notice: Marcus says 'I don't want to hold us up' in turn 11, then in turn 13 starts to raise his point again — 'I still think we should maybe' — and stops himself: 'actually, never mind, it's fine.'
    Harder to spot.

PHASE 1: Student isn't finding flaws
→ "Re-read turns 5 through 8. Does Sofia ever mention anything that could go wrong with tree planting, or is it all good news?"
→ "Follow Marcus through the whole discussion. Does he change his mind, or does he just stop talking about it? Look at turns 11 and 13 closely."
→ "Look at turn 13. Read Marcus's second sentence out loud. What do you notice about how he stops mid-sentence?"

PHASE 2: Student found flaw but stuck on thinking behavior
→ For "They only talked about the good stuff": Narrow to 3: "They only focused on one part and missed the bigger picture" / "They only paid attention to things that agreed with them" / "They believed it because they felt strongly, not because they had proof"
→ "Imagine you're Sofia. You found this incredible story about 350 million trees being planted in one day, and you're so excited to share it. Why might you skip over the parts that aren't as exciting?"
→ For "Someone raised a good point but gave up on it": Narrow to 3: "They just went along with everyone else instead of thinking for themselves" / "They believed it because they felt strongly, not because they had proof" / "They believed it because of who said it, not because the evidence was good"
→ "Imagine you're Marcus. You think the presentation should include the downsides, but Sofia is really confident and excited, and you still have to build the escape room. Why might you just say 'fine' even though you still disagree?"

PHASE 3: Group discussion is stalled [same for every scenario]
→ "Did you all mark the same turns? Look at where you differ."
→ "Someone in your group found something you missed. Take another look."

PHASE 4: Class isn't engaging with AI perspective
→ "Some of you noticed that Sofia only talks about the good parts of reforestation and never mentions the downsides. That's exactly what the AI flagged too. Nice work — what made that stand out to you?"
→ "The AI says Marcus 'went along with the group instead of thinking for himself.' But some of you might say Marcus was just being practical — they had work to do and couldn't argue forever. Both sides, make your case: was Marcus being a pushover, or was he being realistic?"
→ "If nobody caught the abandoned_concern — go back and read turn 13 out loud. Marcus says 'I still think we should maybe — actually, never mind, it's fine.' What does that tell you about whether he really changed his mind?"
→ "Here's a tough one: Sofia offered a compromise — one slide on deforestation at the beginning. Is that a real compromise that addresses Marcus's concern, or did it just sound good enough to end the argument?"
